Handmade Plotter

With the intention of more deeply exploring the material aesthetics of computation, I recently designed, built and programmed my own drawing machine. Rather than purchasing a ready-made plotter, I decided to take a shot at making one for myself to force myself to learn more about the underlying electronics, as well as escaping the environment of the LCD monitor/ digital screen. My plotter uses cartesian coordinate system for positioning the pen, with two discrete axes of movement in a "flatbed" design. Contrary to some popular consumer plotters that combine the X,Y axes into a "T" shape, the axes of my plotter are entirely independent of each other, simplify the construction and stability of the machine, but also carries a conceptual representation of the how information is processed by the computer. The X-axis of my plotter uses a recycled rail from a retired Epson printer and the Y-axis is formed by a MDF panel fixed to two repurposed drawer slides. Both axes are articulated by NEMA stepper motors and driven with an Arduino Uno.

While it would be possible to use a full-fledged computer (such as raspberry pi) to enable more complex control of the plotter like printing vector files or responding to networked inputs, I chose to limit the plotter to only running scripts run directly by a microcontroller. This allows a more direct feeling of drawing when working with the plotter and means each print must be expressed algorithmically.
